🔍 Perplexity 101 ⏱️ 10 min

What it is, why it's different, and how to get the most from it in under 10 minutes.

  • AI answer engine combining live web search + real-time data + reasoning models
  • Returns cited answers — every claim linked to a source, not just a list of links
  • Has modes: Quick Search, Pro Search, Deep Research
  • vs Google: synthesises and reasons rather than just indexing — you get an answer, not ten links
  • vs ChatGPT: always connected to real-time web, always cited, no knowledge cut-off problem
  • Multi-step research agent — reads 20–50 sources before composing its answer
  • Best for: complex questions, competitive analysis, grant research, technical deep-dives
  • Takes 1–3 minutes but saves hours of manual research

Example prompt from real work:

Research 3 UK funding bodies for automotive education CICs. Compare eligibility criteria, funding amounts, and success rates. Provide direct links to application pages.

⚡ Sonar Large

A BOM — short for Bill of Materials — is essentially a shopping list for your project. According to makerspaces.com and RS Components, it lists every component you need: resistors, microcontrollers, connectors, and fasteners. Include part numbers and quantities to simplify ordering.

🧠 Claude 4.5 Sonnet

A Bill of Materials (BOM) is a structured document specifying every component required to build a product. In electronics, a typical BOM includes: component reference designators (R1, C2…), part numbers, manufacturer, quantity, unit cost, and supplier. It serves as the canonical source of truth during procurement, assembly, and revision control. In KiCad, you can export a BOM directly from the schematic editor via the BOM plugin.

✍️ GPT-5

Imagine you're baking a cake — before you start, you'd write out every ingredient you need. A Bill of Materials is exactly that, but for your maker project. It's your official ingredients list: every resistor, every bolt, every chip. Get it right upfront and you'll never find yourself halfway through a build, missing a crucial part. Think of it as your project's passport — it goes everywhere with you.

Quick decision guide:

Quick research? → Sonar | Complex reasoning? → Claude | Writing? → GPT-5
Default (Sonar) is great for most tasks. Switch to Claude for analysis, GPT-5 for writing.

✅ Best Practices ⏱️ 10 min

What separates great AI results from mediocre ones is almost always the human's approach, not the tool.

✅ What Works

  • Be specific: include audience, format, length, tone in every prompt
  • Iterate: treat first drafts as raw material, not final output
  • Use roles: "Act as a grant reviewer" or "Act as a beginner learner"
  • Layer context: Space + file + conversation instructions together
  • Ask it to critique its own work: "What's weak about this?"
  • Provide examples: "Write it like this example paragraph..."

⚠️ Watch Out For

  • Hallucinated statistics — always verify specific numbers from original sources
  • Outdated information — Deep Research helps, but double-check critical facts
  • Generic outputs from vague prompts — garbage in, garbage out
  • Over-relying on first drafts for anything submitted to funders or regulators
  • Losing your authentic voice — edit heavily; it should sound like you
  • Sharing sensitive personal data in prompts — treat AI like a public inbox
  • Think of AI as a very fast, well-read junior colleague — brilliant at research and first drafts, needs your domain expertise and judgement
  • You bring: knowledge of your audience, local context, relationship history, professional judgment
  • AI brings: speed, breadth, patience for repetitive tasks, ability to maintain consistency across long documents
  • The best work happens when you go back and forth — brief it, review it, redirect it, refine it
Pitfall 1: Submitting AI output without editing. Funders and regulators can often tell — and it signals you haven't engaged with your own application.
Pitfall 2: Using AI for decisions it shouldn't make. AI can draft a safeguarding policy but a qualified person must review and sign it off.
Pitfall 3: Starting a new chat every time. Without a Space, you're losing all accumulated context. Set up Spaces before your next project.
Pitfall 4: Trusting statistics without checking. AI will confidently cite numbers that are wrong or outdated. Always trace stats back to primary sources.
You are the director. AI is your research assistant and first-draft writer. Always interrogate the output.

❓ Q&A & Next Steps

Common questions, resources to take home, and what to do next.

Is my data private when I use Perplexity and Comet?
Perplexity Pro users can opt out of having their queries used for training. Don't paste sensitive personal data (client names, financial details, safeguarding records) into any AI tool — treat it like a public inbox. Use anonymised or generalised context instead.
How accurate is the output? Can I trust it?
Perplexity's cited answers are generally reliable for factual research — always click through to verify statistics and legal claims. Comet documents are good first drafts but need your review. AI is your accelerator, not your final authority.
What's the difference between Perplexity Pro and the free version?
Free gives you Quick Search with limited queries. Pro unlocks unlimited Pro Search, Deep Research mode, model choice (Claude, GPT-5), file uploads, Spaces, and Comet access. For serious work use, Pro is worth it at roughly £17/month.
How many Spaces can I create?
Perplexity Pro supports multiple Spaces — create one per major project or client. There's no hard limit advertised; in practice, create one for every context you regularly return to.
